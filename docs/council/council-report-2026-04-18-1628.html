The Question

Pressure-test the overall strategy, product direction, market positioning, technical approach, and go-to-market plan for Showfier — a solo-founder cloud SaaS that translates mixing console show files between brands. MVP deployed (Yamaha CL/QL ↔ DiGiCo SD). DiGiCo writer never validated on a real console. Paddle not yet integrated. One narrow competitor. Finite runway. Is the current plan right, or are key assumptions wrong?

Council Verdict

  • DiGiCo writer is untested on a real console — existential. No validated product until a translated file loads cleanly on an SD12.
  • Trust is the whole business. This is a tiny, brutal, word-of-mouth community. One failure ends it.
  • The 7-tier pricing is broken (5-pack costs more per credit than singles).
  • Roadmap is too ambitious for a solo founder — API, team plans, scene builder, marketplace are distractions until a paying funnel exists.

Where the Council Clashes

  • TAM. Contrarian: realistic ceiling ~$30–60K ARR. Expansionist: $30M ARR + $200M exit. Honest midpoint: $150K–$500K micro-SaaS is plausible; anything more requires B2B channels that don't exist yet.
  • Pricing model. Transactional (credits) vs. subscription vs. rental-seat — right answer depends on who the real buyer turns out to be.
  • Founder allocation. Executor says code Monday. First Principles says be at 20 shows in person. Both are right; they compete for the same calendar.
  • Framing. First Principles says "wrong unit — build a portable show." Executor says "prove the product you have before redesigning it." D is right about the end state; C is right that D's reframe is premature.

Blind Spots the Council Caught (in Peer Review)

All five peer reviewers independently flagged the same gap — something no advisor raised:

  • Liability and the show-day failure mode. What happens when a translated file causes a cancelled show in front of 5,000 people? Refunds don't cover a $250K cancelled gig. The reputational blast radius is the product-ending risk. ToS + liability cap + E&O insurance + explicit "verify before doors" UX must exist before the first Paddle charge.
  • IP / reverse-engineering posture. DMCA §1201(f) and EU Software Directive Art. 6 protect interop, but a 30-minute Delaware tech-lawyer call to confirm ToS drafts is cheap insurance against cease-and-desist.
  • Verification harness. Round-trip testing every output against each manufacturer's own offline editor as continuous CI. Makes the liability caps defensible and neutralizes the firmware-update treadmill.

Gregorio's existing project doc actually mentions E&O at ~$1,100/yr and has disclaimer copy drafted — so this is a planning-to-execution gap, not an awareness gap. Close it before Paddle, not after.

The Recommendation

Follow the Executor's 14-day plan, with three amendments:

  1. Before Paddle: ship the ToS + liability cap + show-day disclaimer + E&O insurance quote. A lawyer call + $1,100/yr premium is not a distraction — it's the enabling condition for taking money.
  2. Before A&H, before any new console: build a round-trip verification harness. Every output auto-reimported to the target console's offline editor, diffed against the source. Technical moat and trust artifact at the same time.
  3. Keep the First Principles reframe on the shelf, not in the build queue. Don't chase "portable show" or "Universal Show File Model as MIDI" before 50 engineers have paid $19. But let the reframe guide who you interview this spring: rental companies and artist management are the B2B channel the council agrees on.

Reject: The Expansionist's $200M exit narrative (fantasy at current stage). The Outsider's demand to rebrand immediately (expensive, already built) — but fix the pricing table today: the 5-pack costing more per credit than singles is an unforced trust-destroying bug. Cut to three tiers: $5 single, $19/mo unlimited, Team on request.

Full Advisor Responses

The Contrarian — Four fatal flaws you're not confronting

1. Your TAM math is fiction. 20K-40K "touring-level engineers" is a fantasy. The real paying market — engineers who hit the problem frequently, don't have a workaround, and trust a cloud tool 2 hours before doors — is 2K-4K globally. At $149/yr ARPU with realistic 5–10% conversion, $30K-$60K ARR ceiling, not $660K. ConsoleFlip being a solo dev isn't opportunity — it's a tell.

2. "Zero tolerance for bugs" isn't a risk — it's the product killing itself. One botched translation at a festival, and "Stop rebuilding your shows" becomes "Showfier cost me my gig." You cannot ship a file-integrity product as a solo non-developer founder on Claude-generated code.

3. Reverse-engineered binary parsers are a treadmill you can't stay on solo. Five console families × firmware updates 2-4x/year = silent mis-mappings. Yamaha pushes CL5 firmware in month 8, HPF frequency mis-maps, and you don't catch it because you're doing a gig in Brazil. Who's on call?

4. The GTM contradicts the product. Forum seeding burns after post #2. Rental companies won't partner with a solo founder with no SLA. API licensing requires enterprise sales muscle you don't have.

The real question: Is this a business, or a $3K/mo side-income tool dressed as a SaaS?

The First Principles Thinker — You're solving the wrong problem

The real problem isn't "my show file is in the wrong format." It's "I just lost 4 hours of prep time and I'm about to mix a show with settings I can't trust." Translation is a symptom. The actual pain is confidence under time pressure. A translated file you can't trust is worse than no file — now you're debugging someone else's output at line check. This is why ConsoleFlip hasn't runaway-won: a $15 file you have to audit anyway is a lottery ticket, not a product.

1. "Show file" is the wrong unit. The unit is the engineer's mental model — a cloud-native, console-agnostic representation of channel intent, not channel bytes. Translation becomes a side effect, not the product. This is PatchPilot, not Showfier. Wrong wedge.

2. SaaS-per-translation is wrong model. Engineers hit this 2–12x/year. Transactional pain, not subscription pain. $19/mo will churn brutally. Real ARR: rental companies (50x/week) or artist/production management.

3. Solo-founder-with-Claude is fine for code, wrong for trust. In live audio, trust is a human name at FOH. Your job isn't "build parsers," it's "be visibly at 20 shows fixing translations in person." Code commoditizes; credibility doesn't.

Build instead: a cloud-hosted, console-agnostic show document ("your show, not your file") monetized via rental-company seats, translation as loss-leader. You're building a file converter. The market wants a portable show.

The Expansionist — Priced like a Chrome extension, actually infrastructure

Gregorio is the only person on Earth with a working Universal Show File Model. That's not a product — that's infrastructure, and he's pricing it like a Chrome extension. The Universal Model IS the asset; translation is just the wedge. Once the model covers the top 6 consoles, he owns the canonical data layer for live audio. This is the "MIDI moment" for mixing consoles — and MIDI became a 40-year standard owned by nobody. Showfier could own it.

  1. iCloud for show files. Every engineer's career in one versioned cloud library, portable across any console forever. $49/mo and lifetime lock-in moat.
  2. The AI audio layer runs on his schema. When someone builds "ChatGPT for mixing" (within 18 months), they need structured show file data. Gregorio is quietly building the only labeled dataset. License it — or become the AI audio company.
  3. Rental companies are distribution, not customers. Clair, Solotech, PRG move 10,000+ files a week. One Clair deal = category dominance. Pursue before SaaS flywheel, not after.
  4. Manufacturer co-option. Sell white-label "import from competitor" to Yamaha/DiGiCo/A&H — they pay to cannibalize each other.

The bet: Stop thinking $3.8M ARR. Think $30M ARR + $200M Audiotonix acquisition in year 4 — they buy the standard, not the revenue. Build the schema like it's the standard. Because it is.

The Outsider — Three seconds in and I can't tell if I'm the customer

"DROP .CLE HERE" — what is a .CLE? An extension I've never seen, into a box, with no explanation. The right-side "preview" is words I don't recognize flowing into other words I don't recognize.

"Stop rebuilding your shows." — Rebuilding what? A show sounds like an event that already happened. Compare: "Switch sound boards in 30 seconds, not 8 hours." I get that instantly.

The brutalist terminal aesthetic fights the trust signal. Yellow-on-black monospace says "hacker side project," not "reliable tool I bet my gig on." The aesthetic is for the founder, not the customer.

Pricing is a maze. The 5-pack is more expensive per credit than buying singles. That's a bug or a trap. Kill three tiers. "One-off $5. Unlimited $19/mo. Team on request." Done.

"First one free" + "1 lifetime" feels stingy — one shot to judge if this works on my files before I'm locked out forever. That's not generous, that's a tripwire.

Market claim is unverified. 20K–40K is a range wide enough to drive a truck through. $400–$2,000/day is the engineer's rate, not the value of hours saved — conflating those is how founders fool themselves.

Would I buy it? I can't tell if I'm the customer. That's the whole problem.
